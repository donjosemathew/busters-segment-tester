Decision logic — edge cases
⚡ Loyal vs At-risk overlap ▶
● Loyal wins. ≥12 orders/yr qualifies for Loyal, but 45–120 days inactivity triggers At-risk too. Volume signal outweighs temporary lapse. Optionally sub-flag as "Loyal-at-risk" for extra care campaigns.
⚡ Re-engaged vs At-risk overlap ▶
● Re-engaged wins. Recent order (≤30 days) after a long lapse (tenure ≥90 days) signals a return intent. Even if gap previously crossed 45 days, the fresh purchase resets the signal. Priority 4 > Priority 5.
⚡ Exact 45-day boundary ▶
● At-risk wins at day 45. Regular requires last order < 45 days (strictly). At-risk starts at ≥ 45 days. If days_since_last = 45 exactly → At-risk. Watch for timezone/midnight boundary bugs in your data pipeline.
⚡ Loyal vs Regular boundary (12 orders) ▶
● Loyal wins at exactly 12. Both rules include 12 orders/yr (Regular: 6–12, Loyal: ≥12). Priority resolves it. Monitor year-boundary resets — if orders/yr drops to 11 next month, customer reclassifies to Regular.
⚡ Dormant vs Lost boundary (269/270 days) ▶
● Strict cutoff at day 270. Dormant = 121–269 days (win-back still viable). Lost = ≥270 days (minimal spend, suppress from main campaigns). A 1-day discrepancy flips the entire strategy. Validate data freshness daily.
⚡ First-time with long inactivity ▶
● First-time always wins (Priority 1). Even if total=1 and days_since=200, the customer is still First-time — not Dormant. No other segment applies until order 2. Consider a separate "abandoned first-timer" flag for long-lapsed single orders.
⚡ Re-engaged with ≥6 total orders ▶
● At-risk or Regular — not Re-engaged. Re-engaged requires total < 6 orders. A customer with 7+ orders returning after a lapse is a lapsed Regular/Loyal, treated differently. They'd classify as At-risk or Regular based on recency.
